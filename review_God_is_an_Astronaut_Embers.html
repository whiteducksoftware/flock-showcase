Three critical snapshots

New Noise Magazine — 3.5/5 (Sept 6, 2024)

Album Review: God Is an Astronaut – “Embers”

Darren Petrosino hears a powerful fusion of retro-psychedelia and GIAA’s signature post-rock, framed by a heartfelt tribute to the Kinsella brothers’ late father. Sitar shades the first half; Jo Quail’s cello deepens the second. Highlights include the propulsive title track, “Falling Leaves,” and “Odyssey.” The critique: the back end loses a bit of its psychedelic glow, with “Prism” under-delivering on its buildup.

Personal, expansive, and at times intoxicatingly nostalgic — with a few pacing dips in the home stretch.
Distorted Sound Magazine — 8/10 (Sept 6, 2024)

ALBUM REVIEW: Embers – God Is An Astronaut

Charlotte Griffiths praises Embers as a “cinematic epic.” Each track feels like its own universe: “Apparition” as moody alt-rock with global textures, “Odyssey” a widescreen ride, “Realms” drifting through sci‑fi darkness. The album transports, escapes, and thrills, with the band’s identity intact.

Transportive, vividly visual post‑rock that feels like scoring your own film.
Hot Press — 8/10 (Sept 6, 2024)

Album Review: God Is An Astronaut, Embers

Edwin McFee calls the Wicklow trio “post‑rock royalty” and finds Embers suitably celestial. With guest turns from Jo Quail (cello) and Dara O’Brien (sitar), the record folds in psych, krautrock, desert rock and prog. Standouts: the ten‑minute title track and the Middle Eastern‑flavoured “Apparition.”

Grand, absorbing, and exhilarating — proof there’s still plenty of fire in the engines.

Review: A telescope made of riffs, grief, and glittering stardust

Embers finds God Is an Astronaut doing what great spacefarers do best: charting new constellations without forgetting where their launchpad is. It’s a record born of loss and lit by memory — you can practically hear the vacuum tubes warming up, the sitar resonating like sunlight on old family photos, and the cello drawing silver filigree across a night sky that refuses to dim.

“Apparition” opens like a mirage on the horizon, and before you can say “fasten seatbelts,” the band folds in global textures so naturally you might suspect a secret portal in their pedalboards. “Falling Leaves” moves from a hush of remembrance to a ragged, joyful roar — the kind you let out when the heart decides grief will share a coffee with gratitude. Then “Odyssey” kicks down the door with a swaggering, wide‑screen stride that makes you want to climb a mountain and invent your own closing credits.

The title track is the album’s event horizon — ten minutes that compresses time, with drums sprinting like comets and synth arpeggios bubbling like a star factory on overtime. Jo Quail’s arrival on “Realms” and “Prism” pulls a veil of cathedral dusk over the second half, a cool‑toned counterpoint to the sitar‑tinted dawn of the first. If there’s a stumble, it’s that “Prism” hints at a cathedral‑toppling finale and instead chooses a graceful curtsy. But when the curtain falls on “Hourglass,” it feels intentional: embers don’t explode; they glow, they persist, and they keep the room warm.

Across two decades, GIAA have refined the rare art of being both colossal and humane. Embers stretches their cosmos with colour and contour — post‑rock that remembers the rock and the post. It’s headphone cinema where the popcorn is your pulse. If you need to escape reality for 55 minutes, this is your wormhole; if you need reality to feel a little bigger, it’s a telescope — the kind you can play through a Vox AC30.

Verdict: Embers is a luminous, cinematic burn — the sound of a band turning grief into propulsion and making the galaxy hum along. 4.5/5 starglow shards.
